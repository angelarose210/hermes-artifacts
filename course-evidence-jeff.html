Will people pay Jeff for a course?

The short answer: you're both right. Nobody pays for information that's free elsewhere. But a course doesn't sell information, it sells the path, the speed, and Jeff's feedback, delivered to the 99% of his audience who can't drive to Ohio for a lesson. That gap is the product.

Jeff's objection, taken seriously

"Why would anyone pay when they can watch me, or a dozen other channels, for free?"

Correct, if the product is "more tips." It isn't. People don't buy a course to learn facts; they buy it to stop guessing. A 3.0 player drowning in free videos doesn't know what to fix next. The course is "do these things, in this order, and I'll check your form." And the "not a famous pro" worry is backwards: amateurs learn better from a certified teacher of regular players than from a top pro who can't explain what they do on instinct. Coaches sell courses. Pros mostly don't. That's the model, not the exception.

The real reason: 99% of his audience can't reach him

Jeff's teaching already exists in two forms. His worldwide audience can reach neither of the good ones.

In person
Structured, hands-on, with real feedback — the version that actually works
Locked to Ohio & Pennsylvania. Unavailable to ~19,900 of his ~20,000 followers.
Free YouTube
Worldwide, but scattered one-off clips with no order and no feedback
By design, it can never tell a viewer what they personally are doing wrong, or in what order to fix it.
The gap
A structured path + Jeff's feedback, delivered anywhere
This is the course. For everyone outside driving distance, it's the only way to buy what Jeff is best at. That's a product, not a paywall on free clips.
This also answers "why don't people comment asking for lessons?" — you don't publicly ask a local coach for lessons when you live 1,000 miles away. That demand goes private. It's exactly why the interest you've seen has come through DMs, not comments. The DMs are the signal.
